Subjunctive Conditionals and Time

A Defense of a Weak Classical Approach

Author: Johan Mårtensson Date: March 27, 2000
Publisher: Acta Universitatis Gothoburgensis ISBN: 91-7346-365-5
Read PDF Download PDF

Abstract

This thesis examines the semantic and logical properties of subjunctive conditional sentences — also known as counterfactuals. It argues that current formal systems of conditional logic do not adequately represent our intuitions about such sentences.

In particular, the following two principles are challenged:

(CSO)   ((A ⇒ B) ∧ (B ⇒ A)) → ((A ⇒ C) ↔ (B ⇒ C))
(CV)   ((A ⇒ C) ∧ (A ⇏ ¬B)) → ((A ∧ B) ⇒ C)

CSO appears in nearly every current axiomatization of conditional logic. The thesis argues its inclusion stems from treating closeness between possible worlds as a fixed, context-relative relation. Instead, an antecedent-relative notion of closeness is proposed, defined in causal terms, and its formal consequences are fully developed.

Contents

  1. Introduction — Historical background on formal semantics of conditionals
  2. Semantics for Conditionals — Truth vs. assertibility, compositionality, extensional, intensional, hyperintensional, and class selection semantics
  3. Closeness and Time — Historical conservativity, the Criterion of Late Departure, future similarity, the Causal Trail Criterion (RCT), cotenability
  4. Axiomatizations — Soundness and completeness for class selection, facts revision, antecedent-relative closeness (AR'), and relative closeness semantics
  5. Summary

Key Contributions

  • Demonstrates that CSO is counter-intuitive and should be rejected
  • Introduces an antecedent-relative notion of world-closeness grounded in causation
  • Develops the Relative Causal Trail (RCT) criterion for world-closeness
  • Proves soundness and completeness of the axiomatic system AR' with respect to Antecedent Relative Relative Closeness (ARRC) semantics
