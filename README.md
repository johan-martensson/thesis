# Subjunctive Conditionals and Time
**A Defense of a Weak Classical Approach**

**Author:** Johan Mårtensson
**Date:** March 27, 2000
**Published by:** Acta Universitatis Gothoburgensis, Göteborg
**ISBN:** 91-7346-365-5 | **ISSN:** 0283-2380

## Abstract

This thesis examines the semantic and logical properties of subjunctive conditional sentences — also known as *counterfactuals*. It argues that current formal systems of conditional logic do not adequately represent our intuitions about such sentences.

In particular, the following principles are challenged:

- **(CSO)** `((A ⇒ B) ∧ (B ⇒ A)) → ((A ⇒ C) ↔ (B ⇒ C))` — a principle present in nearly every current axiomatization of conditional logic
- **(CV)** `((A ⇒ C) ∧ (A ⇏ ¬B)) → ((A ∧ B) ⇒ C)`

The thesis argues that CSO's inclusion stems from a fixed, context-relative notion of *closeness* between possible worlds. Instead, an **antecedent-relative** notion of closeness is proposed, defined in causal terms. The formal consequences are developed into a complete semantics and axiomatic system with desirable intuitive properties.

## Contents

1. **Introduction** — Historical background on formal semantics of conditionals (from Stalnaker 1968 onward)
2. **Chapter 1: Semantics for Conditionals** — Types of conditionals, truth vs. assertibility, compositionality, extensional/intensional/hyperintensional semantics, class selection semantics, and relative closeness semantics
3. **Chapter 2: Closeness and Time** — Historical conservativity, the Criterion of Late Departure, future similarity, causal trail criterion, cotenability, and Bennett's causal chain criterion
4. **Chapter 3: Axiomatizations** — Rules and axiom schemata, canonical semantics, soundness and completeness results for class selection, facts revision, antecedent-relative closeness, and relative closeness semantics
5. **Summary**

---

## Key Contributions

- Demonstrates that the principle CSO is unintuitive and should be rejected
- Introduces an **antecedent-relative** notion of world-closeness grounded in causation
- Develops the **Relative Causal Trail (RCT) criterion** for closeness
- Proves soundness and completeness for the resulting axiomatic system **AR'** with respect to Antecedent Relative Relative Closeness (ARRC) semantics
